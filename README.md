Problem Statement 4: The Emerging Artist<br>
Across communities and institutions, a quiet disconnect is widening between younger 
generations drawn to traditional art forms and the experienced practitioners who carry that 
knowledge. Aspiring artists - curious about folk painting, classical dance, indigenous craft, or 
regional music traditions - find themselves without clear, structured pathways to learn.
Digital learning platforms have democratised access to creative education globally, yet 
traditional knowledge systems remain largely absent from these spaces. Courses in 
contemporary design, digital illustration, and modern performance are abundant. Structured 
curricula for centuries-old art forms are not.
Informal mentorship occasionally bridges this gap. In some communities, dedicated 
practitioners take on students through personal networks, sustaining transmission in 
fragmented and unscalable ways. Elsewhere, aspiring learners with genuine interest find no 
entry point at all — not due to disinterest, but due to absence of accessible infrastructure.
These patterns vary significantly across regions, art forms, and demographics. Some traditional 
disciplines retain active teaching ecosystems while others face near-complete generational 
breaks, with causes that are inconsistently understood.
Multiple overlapping factors contribute — geographic distance between learners and experts, 
absence of structured curricula, language and dialect barriers, incompatibility with modern 
learning formats, and lack of institutional recognition for traditional knowledge. No single 
intervention has reliably resolved the transmission gap across different art forms and contexts.
Participants are expected to build a system capable of connecting aspiring learners with 
traditional experts, structuring knowledge into accessible learning pathways, and adapting 
ancient practices into formats suited to modern engagement — without diluting their cultural 
integrity.
